Agglomerative Clustering¶

Agglomerative clustering is a hierarchical clustering algorithm that builds clusters step by step, starting with individual data points and merging them based on proximity. One of the key advantages of agglomerative clustering is its ability to create a hierarchy, offering a visualization (dendrogram) that shows how clusters are formed at different levels of granularity. This is useful when the user is unsure about the optimal number of clusters since the algorithm doesn't require this parameter upfront.

However, agglomerative clustering can be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets, because it requires calculating distances between all data points at each iteration. Additionally, this method can be sensitive to noise and outliers, often merging them into existing clusters or failing to detect them as anomalies.

KMeans¶

KMeans is a widely used clustering algorithm that excels in its simplicity and speed. It works by iteratively assigning points to the nearest centroid and then recalculating the centroids based on the cluster's mean. This process is repeated until convergence. One of KMeans' main strengths is its efficiency, which makes it well-suited for large datasets. It's also straightforward to implement and tends to perform well on datasets where the clusters are roughly spherical or blob-shaped.

However, KMeans has several limitations. It requires the number of clusters (k) to be specified beforehand, which can be a challenge if the structure of the data is not known in advance. Additionally, the algorithm is sensitive to the initial placement of centroids; poorly initialized centroids can lead to suboptimal clustering. KMeans also assumes that clusters are spherical and evenly sized, which means it struggles with complex shapes like moons, circles, or irregular patterns.

DBScan¶

DBScan (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) is designed to find clusters based on density. This allows it to detect clusters of arbitrary shape, such as moon- or circle-shaped clusters, and handle datasets with noise or outliers effectively. Unlike KMeans, DBScan does not require the number of clusters to be specified in advance. Instead, it relies on two parameters: epsilon (ε), which defines the radius of the neighborhood, and minPts, the minimum number of points required to form a dense region. If points do not belong to any dense region, they are classified as noise.

Despite these strengths, DBScan has its own drawbacks. The algorithm's performance is highly sensitive to the choice of ε and minPts, which can be challenging to determine for datasets with varying densities. DBScan may also struggle when the dataset contains clusters of significantly different densities, as it could fail to distinguish between them or wrongly classify some as noise.

Blob-shaped data refers to clusters that are spherical or circular and well-separated. KMeans is ideal for this type of data, as it assumes clusters are circular and equally spaced, so it can easily detect and separate blob-like clusters. Agglomerative clustering also performs well on blob-shaped data since its distance-based merging method is compatible with this type of clustering pattern. DBScan can handle blob-shaped clusters too, but tuning the ε and minPts parameters properly is key for it to function well in this context.

fig = make_subplots(rows=1, cols=3, shared_yaxes=True, horizontal_spacing=0.01)

kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3)
kmeans.fit(blobs_data)
clusters = kmeans.predict(blobs_data)
blobs['cluster'] = clusters
trace1 = px.scatter(blobs, 'x1', 'x2', 'cluster', width=800, height=500)['data'][0]
fig.add_trace(trace1, row=1, col=1)

agglo = AgglomerativeClustering(n_clusters=3,metric='euclidean')
clusters = agglo.fit_predict(blobs_data)
blobs['cluster'] = clusters
trace2 = px.scatter(blobs, 'x1', 'x2', 'cluster', width=800, height=500)['data'][0]
fig.add_trace(trace2, row=1, col=2)

dbscan = DBSCAN(eps=0.5, min_samples=5)
dbscan.fit(blobs_data)
clusters = dbscan.labels_
blobs['cluster'] = clusters
trace3 = px.scatter(blobs, 'x1', 'x2', 'cluster', width=800, height=500)['data'][0]
fig.add_trace(trace3, row=1, col=3)

fig.update_layout(title='KMeans vs. Agglomerative Clustering vs. DBSCAN - blobs data', 
                  template='plotly_dark', coloraxis = {'colorscale':'viridis'})

pyo.iplot(fig)

Moon-shaped data represents non-spherical clusters with curved patterns. KMeans performs poorly here because it assumes that clusters are spherical and evenly sized, which does not align with the shape of the data. It tends to force boundaries where they don't belong, splitting the curved shapes into arbitrary parts. Agglomerative clustering, on the other hand, can perform decently since it doesn't assume cluster shapes, but it may still have difficulty with closely linked data points. DBScan is the best-suited algorithm for this kind of data, as it can naturally detect the non-spherical, crescent-shaped structures based on density, without assuming a particular form.

Circle-shaped data represents clusters arranged in circular patterns, often with gaps between the points in the circle. KMeans struggles here because it assumes spherical clusters, which results in poor performance; it tends to split the circular data incorrectly into smaller spherical clusters. Agglomerative clustering can perform moderately well, but it may struggle to perfectly outline the circular pattern since it doesn’t inherently capture the shape of such structures. DBScan, once again, excels in this scenario. It is capable of detecting non-spherical clusters and can easily recognize the continuous circular boundary due to its density-based nature.

In datasets with random patterns, where there is no clear structure or cluster formation, all algorithms face challenges. Agglomerative clustering might find some structure, but it's likely to result in clusters that don't have meaningful groupings. KMeans, by forcing clusters, will also generate arbitrary groupings with little significance. DBScan, depending on the level of noise, will often classify most of the points as noise, identifying only one or possibly none at all. In highly random data, DBScan’s ability to separate noise from clusters can be an advantage, but all algorithms generally struggle with purely random data.
